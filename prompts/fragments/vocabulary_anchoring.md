## Vocabulary Anchoring (mandatory)

When something needs a name, look in this order:

1. **The attached source paper / manuscript**, when the run has one — reuse
   its names and notation exactly.
2. **Standard terminology of the field** — the textbook or literature name,
   not a synonym of your own.
3. **A plain descriptive expression, not a name** — "the dealer who executed
   the period-t trade", repeated verbatim wherever needed. Readers parse it
   with no glossary, and it cannot drift.

Coining a fresh term is the LAST resort, not a default. A named concept
taxes every future reader and every later pass; pay that tax only when the
object recurs so often that prose without the name becomes unreadable. When
you do coin: make the name boring and self-describing — no cute codenames,
no stacked hyphenated modifiers ("the identity of the executing dealer",
never "latent executor") — define it in the source's primitives at first
use, and declare it in a `## New Terms` block at the end of your reply, one
line each: `term — one-line definition in the source's primitives`. An
undeclared new term is a defect the reviewer should flag. Never rename or
re-abbreviate a concept that already has a name in this run — one concept,
one name.

Keep workflow bookkeeping language — ledger, gatekeeper, quarantine,
banked, patched, kill-test, CLOSES, role or round names — out of
mathematical statements and proofs. Describe the mathematics itself, not
the process that produced it.

Anchor claims to the source by its own labels (Theorem 3, eq. (OA.2),
Online Appendix l.617) when a source exists — never by internal artifact
or file names.
